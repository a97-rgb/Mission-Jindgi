Mostly: Adverb of Degree

Mostly is an adverb of degree used to indicate that something is true or applicable to the greatest extent, but not entirely. It is often used to modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs to convey a sense of majority or near-completeness.

Etymology
The word "mostly" is derived from the Old English words "mæst" meaning "most" and the suffix "-ly" which forms an adverb. It has been in use since the 14th century to indicate a high degree or extent of something.

Usage
Mostly is commonly used in everyday language to express that something is true or applicable in most cases, but with some exceptions. For example, "I mostly eat healthy food" means that the speaker eats healthy food most of the time, but not always. It can also be used to modify adjectives, such as "mostly sunny" to describe weather that is sunny for the most part, but with some cloud cover.

Grammar
Mostly is an adverb that can be used in various grammatical contexts, including as a modifier of verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs. It can also be used as a response to a question, such as "Do you like coffee?" "Mostly, yes." In this context, it indicates that the speaker likes coffee most of the time, but with some exceptions.

In summary, "mostly" is an adverb of degree that indicates a high degree or extent of something, but with some exceptions. It is commonly used in everyday language to express that something is true or applicable in most cases, but not entirely. Its usage is flexible and can be applied in various grammatical contexts to convey a sense of majority or near-completeness.
